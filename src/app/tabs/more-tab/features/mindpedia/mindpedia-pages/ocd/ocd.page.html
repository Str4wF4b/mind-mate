Obsessive Compulsive Disorder

What is it?

The lives of people with OCD are characterised by everyday obsessive thoughts and actions. Such thoughts often trigger fear and anxiety. In order to neutralise these imagined dangers, affected people perform adapted, typically exaggerated actions.

Symptoms

Obsessive Thoughts:


  • Intrusive, recurring and uncomfortable thoughts or impulses
  • Exaggerated content of thoughts leads to fear, anxiety, shame or disgust
  • Thoughts are often about possible dangers
  • People affected usually know that the thought is their own and exaggerated
  • People affected therefore tend to ignore or suppress these thoughts

Compulsive Behavior:


  • Is performed as a reaction to the obsessive thoughts
  • Is used to avert danger or reduce discomfort (e.g. excessive washing, tidying)
  • Actions are typically exaggerated and have an impact on the affected person's life (mainly due to the amount of time required, e.g. repeatedly washing hands to make sure they are not contaminated)

Avoidance Behavior:


  • Avoiding situations that may cause obsessive thoughts or actions

Reassurance Behavior:


  • Passing on responsibility to involved persons (e.g. other person locks door)
  • Calming by involved persons (e.g. reassuring that no one was injured while driving)

Neutralization:


  • Is performed in thoughts or behavior
  • Is used to equalise or eliminate insecurities (e.g. suppressing thoughts or distraction) 1

Epidemiology

(= incidence of diseases in the population)

Progression:


  • Progression-Start:
    • Mostly: gradual start
    • Rarely: acute onset of symptoms in childhood 2 3
  • Chronic progression with a duration of 9-10 years
  • Early onset, if at the age of about 11 years
  • Late onset, if at the age of approx. 23 years: higher symptom severity than early onset 2
  • Up to 90%: other comorbid (= concurrent) disorder (e.g. anxiety disorder, affective disorder) 4

Risk Factors:


  • Genetics:
    • Heredity: about 48% 5
    • Inheritance through person with early onset of disease: 32.5 times increased risk 6
    • Inheritance through person with late onset of disease: 5 times increased risk 7
  • Environmental Risk Factors:
    • Complications at birth (e.g. low birth weight): high risk
    • Experiencing critical life events (e.g. family problems): possible high risk
    • Advanced parental age: possible high risk 8
    • Certain infections: high risk 9
  • Psychological Risk Factors:
    • Change of information processing (e.g. perfectionism, overestimation of danger and thoughts) 10
    • Psychodynamic Theories: dysfunctional defence mechanisms such as reaction formation 11
    • Origin of contents of obsessive thoughts: Majority reports similar evolutionarily influenced survival strategies 12
    • Cognitive-behavioural Theories: neutral stimulus triggers fear and thus avoidance behaviour is developed 13

Additional Info:


  • 184 million (107.2 million in Europe) people diagnosed with OCD
  • Lifetime prevalence (= cases of illness in a lifetime): 2.3% 4
  • Highest incidence on average at the age of 19.5 years (and before the age of 35) 2

Possible Treatment

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy:


  • Psychoeducation:
    • Patient is informed in detail about the disorder
    • A disorder model can be used to analyse the connection between obsessive thoughts and actions, in combination with the maintenance of the disorder
  • Exposure with Reaction Prevention:
    • Collecting the situations that trigger obsessive thoughts and actions
    • Hierarchical assessment of the difficulty of coping
    • Confrontation with triggers of obsessive thoughts without being able to perform countermeasures (e.g. avoidance, neutralisation) 14
  • Treatment of Dysfunctional Judgements:
    • Changing the way of dealing with one's own thoughts and controlling them
    • Through behavioural experiments (e.g. thought experiment with obsessive thoughts) the thought is questioned and changed 15

Drug Therapy:


  • Administration of Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) 16
  • Augmentation in antipsychotic medication possible if SSRI is insufficient or does not help at all 17
