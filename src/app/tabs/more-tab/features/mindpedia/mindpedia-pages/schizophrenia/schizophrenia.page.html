Schizophrenia

What is it?

Schizophrenia is a mental illness that is subordinate to psychotic disorders and is characterised by the grouping of disorders that are subject to a similar symptom pattern.
In general, a distinction is made between positive or plus symptoms and negative or minus symptoms. However, it is possible that both forms occur simultaneously.

Symptoms

Positive or Plus Symptoms:

This means, experience is increased compared to healthy people.


  • Delusions (e.g. persecutory delusions, delusions of reference, delusions of grandeur, erotomanic delusions)
  • Hallucinations (e.g. hearing voices)
  • Disorganized thinking (e.g. loosening of associations, flight of ideas, tangential speech)
  • Disorganized behaviour (e.g. significant agitation, grimacing)
  • Disturbed motor behavior (e.g. stereotyped movements, catatonic stupor, mutism)
 Negative or Minus Symptoms:

This means, functions are reduced or completely lost compared to healthy people.


  • Reduction in facial expression and gestures
  • Loss of pleasure and motivation
  • Decrease in verbal communication
  • Anhedonia (= restricted or cancelled perception of positive emotions) 1

Epidemiology

(= incidence of diseases in the population)

Progression:


  • Symptoms of schizophrenia last for at least a year
  • very different progressions:
    • Only a psychotic episode with following complete remission (= abatement of symptoms)
    • Chronic progressive (= worsening) clinical picture without symptom-free phases 2

Risk Factors:


  • Genetic Risk Factors:
    • Heredity: 81% 3
    • Newer and larger studies: 64% 4
  • Psychosocial Risk Factors:
    • Study: Physical trauma (e.g. massive violence, accidents) or other traumatic events (e.g. bullying, childhood neglect) led in 86% of cases to psychotic disorder (i.e. delusions, hallucinations, etc.). 5 6
    • The more traumatic events, the more delusional thoughts 7
    • (Critical) life events: no significant changes 8
    • Everyday stressors (e.g. missing the bus): small fluctuations of current positive symptoms 9
  • Further Environmental Risk Factors:
    • Pregnancy and birth complications: increased risk 10
    • Season of birth: slightly increased risk for winter-spring births 11

Additional Info:


  • 26 million (4.4 million in Europe) people diagnosed with Schizophrenia 12
  • Lifetime prevalence (= cases of illness in a lifetime): 0.3% Schizophrenia, 0.2% delusions 1
  • Highest incidence at the age of 20-29 (women up to 39) 2
  • Influencing factors: migration, city, level of development of the country 13

Possible Treatment

  • Currently recommended: drug therapy with neuroleptics in acute phase and for relapse prophylaxis (= measure to prevent recurrence of the disease) 14
  • Follow-up recommendation: cognitive behavioural therapy or psychoeducational family interventions 15 16 17
  • Further recommendations: social skills training, neuropsychological training, sociotherapy 17 11 18
